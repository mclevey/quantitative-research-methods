LAB OVERVIEW

A paragraph or two summarizing the lab content and learning objectives.

Keywords

list, of, key, concepts

This lab is organized into three thematic sections: Think, Do, and Debrief.

  1. 🤔 Think consists of open-ended questions designed to spark reflection, critical and creative thinking, design thinking, and problem-solving.
  2. 🤘 Do consists of practical tasks and research problems that build essential quantitative research skills, such as installing and using research software, planning analyses, simulating or processing data, creating graphs, fitting statistical models, and interpreting results.
  3. 👋 Debrief contains prompts to encourage metacognition and growth by providing opportunities to assess your learning, share insights, and discuss challenges and breakthroughs with your lab group.

I recommend that you start working on this material a day (or two) before the lab session. Follow the instructions as best as you can and complete as much as possible. If you encounter any difficulties, make note of them. We will address them during the next class or lab session.

The content in this course is challenging for everyone at first, so feeling confused or frustrated is entirely normal. 😮‍💨 Our active learning approach might amplify these feelings by highlighting things we haven’t yet mastered. Though it can feel uncomfortable, this is a natural and valuable part of learning. Discomfort is useful feedback that tells us where we need to focus our efforts to learn more deeply and durably (Brown, Roediger, and McDaniel 2014; Sloman and Fernbach 2018; Zakrajsek 2022). Take a deep breath, and if needed, stretch or move around. Remember, I’m here to help you learn, and so are your peers. You’ve got this! ✌️

Part 3: Debrief

Part 3 Grade Weight

I expect you to engage with these tasks / learning activities in small groups, but participation is ungraded. The more you put in, the more you get out.

The lab session will include time for discussion. Be prepared to share some of your responses to the questions in Part 1: Think and your work on problems in Part 2: Do with members of your lab group. You should also be prepared to debrief your learning process more generally, for example by discussing challenges or breakthroughs that you, or others in your lab group, are experiencing.

Debriefing is a structured process designed to reflect on the learning experience itself. In this case, debriefing sessions will focus on processing what has just happened during the lab with explicit consideration of the learning objectives or real-world implications.
